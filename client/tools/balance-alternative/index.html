Looking for a Balance App Alternative?

If you're searching for a Balance app alternative, it's worth being upfront: Balance is a well-established, clinically-recognized app, and switching away from it isn't the only option — plenty of people use it alongside another tool rather than replacing it. This page is about the second option: what Balance does well, two things its own users commonly ask for in public reviews, and how Ripple approaches those two areas differently. It's a comparison to help you decide what you actually need, not a case against Balance.

What Balance does well

Balance has real, verifiable credentials: it was the first menopause app to receive ORCHA certification, it's listed in NHS digital health libraries, it's recognized as an Apple Editors' Choice, and it's used by more than a million people worldwide. Its free tier includes a symptom, period, sleep, and mood tracking journal, quarterly symptom questionnaires, and 1-, 3-, and 6-month symptom graphs. A 2026 update means journal logs now feed directly into the Health Report, with an editable symptom summary before you generate it — turning your own entries into a shareable clinician Health Report. An optional paid subscription, Balance+, adds content collections, live expert Q&A sessions, and enhanced symptom and report features, with a 14-day trial.

Two things Balance users commonly ask for

Public reviews of Balance surface two requests that come up often. Neither is a defect — Balance is a mature, well-regarded product, and every tracking app makes trade-offs about where to focus. These are simply the two most commonly requested additions in public reviews as of 2026.

Connecting data from other apps and devices. Balance's tracking is entirely manual — there's currently no integration with Apple Health, Garmin, or MyFitnessPal, so if you already log sleep, activity, or food elsewhere, you end up entering some of it twice.

A view that connects patterns across symptoms. Balance's symptom graphs show each symptom's trend individually over 1, 3, or 6 months, but there's no built-in view that shows how symptoms move together or cluster over time.

How Ripple approaches these two areas

Data from other apps and devices. Ripple's tracking is also manual today — like Balance, it doesn't currently connect to Apple Health, Garmin, or MyFitnessPal. If avoiding duplicate entry across those tools is your main reason for looking around, neither app solves that yet.

Connecting patterns across symptoms. This is where Ripple's approach differs. Ripple's Trigger Tracker looks for correlations between logged triggers and specific symptoms, both same-day and next-day, and flags them by confidence level once you have at least 14 days of co-logged data. Its Biological Correlations view charts two related signals — like sleep duration and heart rate variability — on the same time-series chart, so you can see whether they move together. Both are available inside Ripple today, not on a roadmap.

When your symptoms don't get taken seriously

Perimenopause symptoms are famously easy to mistake for something else. Hot flashes and night sweats are the most recognisable, but brain fog, joint pain, mood swings, heart palpitations, and disrupted sleep can all show up years before periods become irregular — and each one, taken alone, can look like ordinary stress or ageing rather than part of a single underlying pattern. Many women describe being told a symptom is anxiety, being offered antidepressants without any discussion of hormones, being told they're too young for perimenopause, or being sent away because a single hormone blood test came back within a normal range. This experience — raising a symptom, receiving an explanation that doesn't quite fit, and leaving without a clear next step — is a widely reported feature of perimenopause care, not a personal failing on either side of the conversation. Clinicians are working from a few minutes of information in a single appointment; you're the only person who sees the whole pattern over months. That mismatch is exactly what a written record is for.

What the Dismissal Tracker does

The Dismissal Tracker is a simple log, not a diagnostic tool. Each entry records the date of an appointment, the clinic and clinician, the symptoms you reported, what the clinician said or did in response, and whether the concern was later resolved. In Ripple's full app, entries like these are encrypted and stored in your private vault, and they feed automatically into two other tools: Appointment Prep, which builds a printable pre-visit summary, and the Evidence Engine, which assembles a structured clinical brief you can generate before an appointment. The free version on this page works the same way but doesn't require an account — your entries stay in your browser for this session, and you can print or copy them whenever you're ready.

What to record, and when

The most useful time to log an entry is right after the appointment, while the details are still fresh — what you said, what came back, and whether it felt like your concern was actually investigated. Be specific rather than general: "raised hot flashes and brain fog, was told to reduce caffeine and come back in six months" is more useful later than "the doctor wasn't helpful." Note the response in the clinician's own words where you can remember them. Over two or three appointments, a pattern usually becomes visible — the same symptom being minimised in different ways, or a hormone test being used to close a conversation rather than open one.

How a structured record changes a consultation

A single frustrating appointment is easy for a subsequent clinician to read as an isolated event. A dated log of several appointments, each with the symptoms reported and the response given, reads differently — it shows a consistent pattern over time, which is often exactly what helps support a more thorough assessment, a referral, or a second opinion. It also changes the shape of the conversation itself: instead of trying to reconstruct how you felt from memory, you can point to specific dates and specific responses, which keeps the discussion concrete and makes it easier for a new clinician to pick up where the last conversation left off.

How this tool protects your privacy

This page is a genuinely private tool: every entry you type is held in your browser only, for the current session. Nothing is transmitted to a server while you use this page — you can confirm this yourself by opening your browser's developer tools and watching the network tab. Printing or copying your log also happens entirely on your device. Because entries aren't saved between visits, print or copy your log before you close the page if you want to keep it.

Frequently asked questions

What is the Dismissal Tracker?

The Dismissal Tracker is a free tool for keeping a dated record of appointments where your symptoms were dismissed, minimised, or not fully investigated. Each entry captures the clinic, the symptoms you reported, and what the clinician said or did in response, so you have a clear account to refer back to.

Is this a medical or diagnostic tool?

No. It's a record-keeping tool, not a diagnostic one. It doesn't assess your symptoms, suggest a diagnosis, or recommend treatment — it simply helps you keep an accurate, dated account of your own appointments to share with a clinician.

Does this tool send my entries anywhere?

No. Everything you type on this page stays in your browser for the current session. Nothing is transmitted to, or stored on, any server — you can verify this yourself by checking your browser's network tab while you use it. If you close or refresh the page, your entries are gone unless you've printed or copied them first.

What should I write in the "clinician's response" field?

As close to their own words as you can remember — for example, "said my hormone levels were normal and there was nothing more to investigate," rather than a general summary like "wasn't helpful." Specific wording is more useful in a future appointment than a general impression.

How is this different from just describing my symptoms to my doctor?

It gives you a dated, structured account rather than a memory. A pattern across three appointments — the same symptom, minimised in different ways — is easy to describe when you have the dates and details written down, and much harder to reconstruct from memory months later.

Can I use this if I only have one appointment to log?

Yes. There's no minimum — logging a single appointment still gives you a written record you can refer back to, and you can add more entries later if it happens again.

What does Ripple do with the Dismissal Tracker?

Nothing, on this page — your entries stay in your browser and are only saved if you print or copy them yourself. Ripple's full app includes the same tracker as part of an encrypted, ongoing symptom record, where entries can also feed into an appointment-preparation summary and a structured clinical brief. Using this free tool doesn't require an account.
